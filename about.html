Background

I'm a physics student at UTEC, working on a graduate thesis titled “Modelado Computacional de la reparación del daño al ADN por radionúclidos emisores alfa a baja tasa de dosis mediante un enfoque Monte Carlo Multiescala” — computational modeling of DNA damage repair from low-dose-rate alpha-emitting radionuclides, using a multiscale Monte Carlo approach.

The core idea is to couple two very different scales of simulation. On one side, OpenTOPAS and TOPAS-nBio handle the physics: tracking how alpha particles from radionuclides like Actinium-225 deposit energy as they pass through tissue. On the other, an agent-based cellular model built with CompuCell3D's Cellular Potts Model handles the biology: how individual cells respond to that damage over time. The TOPAS-Tissue framework is what lets the two talk to each other.

A lot of the current work centers on the Ac-225 decay chain itself — it doesn't decay in one step, but through a sequence of daughter isotopes, each with its own energy and branching fraction. Getting that right, via the Bateman equations and secular equilibrium, matters because it determines exactly how much energy actually lands where. On the biology side, I'm using DNA double-strand break and DSB-cluster classification to compare simulation output against what's expected at a given radiation quality.

Alongside the thesis, I support the Biological Systems course, building bioinformatics practical exercises for students, and I'm active with AFINPA, UTEC's nuclear and particle physics student group.
